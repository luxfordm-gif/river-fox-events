01 — Brand essence
Page 03
Chapter 01

Considered,
design-led, calm.

River Fox Events is Laura Hyde's luxury event styling studio in Surrey. Balloon installations, backdrops, signage, light-up letters, props and tablescape architecture for milestone moments — christenings, big birthdays, anniversaries, weddings, corporate launches and brand activations.

A · What we promise

A room that feels considered
the moment guests walk in.

Scale and palette held together by one designer's eye, executed with the calm of someone who has done it before. Hosts get something quietly extraordinary — never punchy, never novelty-led.

B · Who we serve

Milestones, mostly.

  • Children's parties — first birthdays through to teenage milestones
  • Milestone celebrations — 30ths, 40ths, 50ths, 60ths and beyond
  • Weddings, christenings, baby showers
  • Corporate & brand styling, award nights, product launches, shopfronts
  • Luxury picnics & outdoor occasions
C · Brand pillars

Three things we hold to.

  1. Design-led. Every brief begins with a render board — palette, scale and sightlines decided before a single balloon is inflated.
  2. Quiet luxury. Pastel and neutral palettes, generous scale, restraint. Never loud. Never themed for the sake of theme.
  3. Calmly delivered. Install morning is steady, not frantic. Hosts hand over the day and stop worrying about it.
D · The rebrand

From Lollipop Balloons to River Fox.

River Fox Events is the rebrand of Lollipop Balloons — the same designer, same inventory, more grown-up positioning. The historic name is preserved in JSON-LD as alternateName so legacy links and brand searches keep flowing through. Don't bury the heritage; don't lead with it either.

02 — Voice & tone
Page 04
Chapter 02

Quietly
authoritative.

Laura speaks as the designer, in first person, with the calm of someone who has done it before. Sentences earn their length. Sensory detail does the work that adjectives can't.

A · Six rules

How Laura sounds.

  1. First person, quietly authoritative. "I want the family to be able to read it in seconds." Not "we" — Laura speaks as the designer.
  2. Short, specific, occasionally fragmentary. "Beautiful bones, very little atmosphere." A two-word sentence is fine when the moment calls for it.
  3. Sensory detail over adjectives. Name the actual thing — "pink scallop-edge plates, a soft zebra-print salad plate on top" — not "beautiful tablescape".
  4. Trade-craft language used naturally. Sightlines, scale, the brief, sourcing, install morning, sign-off, render board. Trust the reader to keep up.
  5. Em dashes welcome. Exclamation marks never. Use the em dash to slow a sentence down, not the exclamation to speed it up.
  6. Quiet expertise, never sales. Explain why a choice was made; don't tell the reader it was excellent.
B · Tone by surface

Same voice, different rooms.

03 — Vocabulary
Page 05
Chapter 03

Say this,
not that.

A · Banned

Never use.

Filler phrases and adjective-led praise that say nothing about the work. Strip on sight.

If you're looking for… · Look no further… · We pride ourselves on… · Our team of experts… · The perfect party · Stunning · Amazing · Wow factor · Show-stopping · Exclamation marks
B · Capped — restraint

Use, but rarely.

  • Bespoke — banned from titles, max once per meta description
  • Luxury — max twice across all page titles site-wide
  • From £460 — appears in max six page metas site-wide
C · Preferred — house language

The trade-craft we like.

Considered · Design-led · Calm · Restraint · Render board · Sightlines · Scale · Install morning · Sign-off · The brief · Sourcing · Tablescape · Threshold piece · Pastel & neutral · Milestone
D · Voice in the wild

Don't, then do.

Don't
"If you're looking for the perfect party, look no further — our team of experts will create a stunning, show-stopping event!"
Filler stack, adjective-led praise, exclamation. Says nothing about the work. Sounds like every other party stylist.
Do
"A pastel palette, a 4m balloon install above the cake table, and a banquet of ten — held together by one designer's eye."
Sensory specifics. Names the actual moves. Quietly states scale without claiming "wow factor".
Don't
"We're so excited to share this amazing styling we did last weekend!"
"We" instead of Laura. "Amazing" carries no information. Reads like a sales post, not a designer's note.
Do
"Beautiful bones, very little atmosphere — a Cobham garden room asking for scale and a softer palette."
Diagnoses the venue. Names the design tension. Reader can already picture the answer.

04 — Aesthetic gate
Page 06
Chapter 04

What we do.
What we don't.

Two filters protect the brand: the work we'll publish images of, and the inventory we actually supply. If a topic needs an out-of-scope item to land, the topic is wrong — pick a different one.

A · Auto-reject

Never published.

  • Soft play setups
  • Mascot or costume-character appearances
  • Disco lights, smoke machines, club-style lighting rigs
  • Phone parties / mobile gaming-truck parties
  • Plastic novelty tat, party-shop bunting, off-the-shelf themed kits
  • Anything cheap-feeling, punchy or novelty-led
B · Discouraged but allowed

Range extension, not the house style.

Only published when there's a real, photographed event behind it — and the design moves (palette, scale, restraint) lead, not the IP. Aim for ≤ 1 in 6 articles in this bucket.

  • Character themes — Bluey, Mario, Disney, Paw Patrol, etc.
  • Neon palettes & brighter colour schemes
C · House style — auto-include

The territory.

  • Pastel and neutral palettes
  • Large-scale installs (4m+ backdrops, banquet tables of 10+)
  • Tablescape-led design
  • Milestone moments — christenings, big birthdays, anniversaries, baby showers, corporate
  • Render-led process pieces
  • Named real venues in Surrey, the South East and London

04 — Aesthetic gate (cont'd)
Page 06b
Chapter 04 · continued

Laura's toolkit.

The canonical in-and-out list. Articles, image direction and design-move language must build only from the IN list. If a topic needs an OUT item to land, the topic is wrong — pick another.

A · IN — Laura supplies

In scope.

  • Balloons — arches, garlands, walls, hoops, towers, organic installs, centrepieces, threshold pieces, number stacks, jumbo & helium
  • Backdrops — fabric, sailboard (custom-painted with vinyl), themed photo walls, sequin walls, full themed installations
  • Signage — painted boards, vinyl & acrylic stickers on board, life-size cardboard cutouts, personalised acrylic and foam signs, custom welcome signs (weatherproof variants)
  • Light-up letters & numbers, number stacks
  • Up-lighters — including waterproof for outdoor
  • Themed props — 3D acrylic, plinths, risers, cake stands (NOT the cake)
  • Faux florals — sparingly, as texture, never the headline
  • Textiles — muslin, cheesecloth, drapery, linens, chair sashes & covers
  • Soft seats, low tables, cushions — kids' setups, picnics
B · OUT — never frame as Laura's work

Out of scope.

  • Fresh florals, fresh foliage, any compositional flower-arranging
  • Vessels, glassware, vintage stoneware (the curated-tablescape category)
  • Candles or candle-holders
  • The cake itself (cake stands only)
  • Catering, food, drink, bar service
C · The floral rule

Laura is not a florist.

Never lead a design move with florals — fresh or faux. Faux stems are a quiet prop, never the subject of an image, headline, pull quote or section title. If anything flower-shaped appears on her displays, it's faux, ambient, and never the focal point.

If a topic seems to need florals to land, the topic is wrong for Laura — pick a different one.

08 — Imagery direction
Page 10
Chapter 08

Editorial,
natural light.

A · House style

The visual anchor.

Photorealistic editorial event styling photography, soft natural daylight, pastel and neutral palette, considered composition, balloon installations and tablescapes in real UK venue interiors, no people or faces, no text, no logos, shot on medium format with shallow depth of field.
B · Subject & composition

What's in the frame.

  • Lead with installations Laura actually supplies — balloons, backdrops, signage, light-up letters, plinths, tablescape props
  • Faux florals can sit in frame as quiet texture, never as the subject
  • Subject centred so a square or 4:3 crop still leads with the subject (hero strips and OG cards crop hard)
  • No people, no faces, no logos, no on-image text
  • Natural daylight feel even when retouched. Shadows soft, never theatrical
C · File & size targets

What to ship.

UseFormat · sizeMax KBLoading
HeroWebP · 1600×900500 KBeager · fetchpriority high
Gallery / supportingWebP · ~1200px wide200 KBlazy
ThumbnailWebP · 800×800100 KBlazy
OG cardWebP · 1200×630200 KB—

Lossy WebP at quality 75–85. srcset with at least three sizes so smaller viewports don't pull the desktop file.

D · Filenames & alt text

One image, one filename, one alt.

  • The rule is per file, not per placement
  • Filename describes content, not the page topic — cobham-balloon-arch-pastel.webp, not journal-image-1.webp
  • Alt text describes what's in the image, not the page or the brand. Max 125 characters
  • Two different files must not open their alt with the same five words
  • Each page needs a per-page OG image in public/og/ overriding the global fallback

Alt text — don't, then do.

Don't
"Children's party styling in Surrey by River Fox Events"
Brand-led, page-led, says nothing about the photo. Repeated across files = duplicate content.
Do
"Pastel balloon arch over a long banquet table set with pink scallop-edge plates, Cobham garden room"
Names the actual subject, palette and venue. Each photo gets its own specific alt.
